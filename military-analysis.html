Data Source:¶

I used the The World Bank Database for data on the countries' military spending, GDP, and population. A lot of the World Bank data actually comes from the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI), which would have been another good source to use. However, I found it easier to use the World Bank since the datasets were already pretty clean and it was easy to navigate around the database to find what I needed.

  • Military Expenditure: https://data-worldbank-org/indicator/MS.MIL.XPND.CD
  • GDP: https://data-worldbank-org/indicator/NY.GDP.MKTP.CD
  • Population: https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/SP.POP.TOTL

#Creating a pivot table using the df_clean dataframe and the 'Indicator Name' and 'Country Name' as indexes
df_clean = pd.pivot_table(df_clean,index=['Indicator Name','Country Name'])

#Using transpose to makes rows into columns and columns into rows - making each country name a column
#and a year column, rather than each year being a seperate column a
mil_df = df_clean.loc['Military expenditure (current USD)'].T.reset_index()
#renaming the index column as year 
mil_df.rename(columns={'index':'Year'}, inplace=True) 
#using melt to change the dataframe from wide to long format - creates 3 columns for year, country, and value
mil_df_new = pd.melt(mil_df, id_vars=['Year'],var_name=['Country Name']) 
#renaming value column so we know what this value is displaying 
mil_df_new.rename(columns={'value':'Military Expenditure'}, inplace=True)  


#I go on to the same exact thing to the gdp data and population data as well to get everything ready to be merged. 
gdp_df = df_clean.loc['GDP (current US$)'].T.reset_index()
gdp_df.rename(columns={'index':'Year'}, inplace=True)
gdp_df_new = pd.melt(gdp_df, id_vars=['Year'],var_name=['Country Name'])
gdp_df_new.rename(columns={'value':'GDP'}, inplace=True)
    
pop_df = df_clean.loc['Population, total'].T.reset_index()
pop_df.rename(columns={'index':'Year'}, inplace=True)
pop_df_new = pd.melt(pop_df, id_vars=['Year'],var_name=['Country Name'])
pop_df_new.rename(columns={'value':'Population'}, inplace=True)

#Merging the three reshaped data frames together and setting the Country Name and Years as the indexes, as well as
#sorting the indexes. 
data_merge = pd.merge(pd.merge(mil_df_new, gdp_df_new), pop_df_new)
data_merge = data_merge.set_index(['Country Name','Year']).sort_index()

Future Predictions:¶

With all of these countries that we're looking at being hit hard by Covid-19 in 2020, this year along with the next few years, will be especially interesting to look at when it comes to military expenditure and GDP. I think that GDP will definitely decrease across all of these countries. Military spending on the other hand can take two paths. The first one, and the most logical one, is that following a crash in GDP and their economies being hit hard, countries will have to decrease their military spending. The second path, and the one that takes into account other variables, is that in light of Covid-19, countries will actually feel the need to increase (or stay consistant with where they're at right now) their military spending due to possible competition and security threats that the virus brought along. For example, we have the US and China who have increased tensions between the two since the start of Covid-19.

Also, I think it's important to mention for the US specifically the fact that we are in the middle of an election and the outcome of this will be a variable in the amount that the US decides to put towards military spending. This is important to highlight because the US makes up a very high portion of the total military spending across the globe.

Although it's hard to tell what will happen, I thought it would be interesting to look at what military spending was like during/after the 2007-2009 Recession, since this time period is probably the most comparable to what we are seeing the economy go through at the moment.

Conclusion/Learning Processes:¶

In conclusion, there are a lot of different perspectives to look at when comparing military spending between countries. If you were to only look at total military expenditure, you would think that countries like the US and China spend an immense amount of money on military spending, which they do - but if you take in to account their GDP, their military expenditure hardly makes a dent in that (especially with China). On the other hand we have countries like Saudi Arabia and Russia whose overall spending is on the lower end yet when compared to their GDP they are actually putting in far more than China and the US.

I think it's also important to note that when comparing data like this, you have to take into account what was going on in the country at the time. Looking at this data and not having any background or reasoning as to why military spending might have been exceptionally high at one point in time for a particular country makes it a lot harder to understand and present a thorough analysis.

As for my learning processes when it comes to data mining and analysis specifically, I learned that there are many different ways you can present data and you really have to play around with what way makes the most sense with the information you're trying to get across. There are no standard 'rules' per say so it really is just trying to find the most clear and understandable way to present the data so that it is useful to your audience.
